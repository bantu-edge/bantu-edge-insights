There is a particular kind of frustration that builds quietly in climate and development work. You have done the research. The report is rigorous. The data is credible. And then the report gets filed. The community moves on. The evidence didn't travel.

I have watched this happen across different contexts — in just energy transition research in Mpumalanga, in urban resilience work in Beira and Durban, in socio-economic assessments along the renewable energy corridors of the Northern Cape. In each case, the research was technically sound. In each case, the distance between evidence and action remained stubbornly wide.

This is not primarily a communication problem, though communication matters. It is a structural one — rooted in how we design research, who we imagine as its audience, and what we count as legitimate knowledge in the first place.

The Architecture of Disconnection

Climate research in Southern Africa tends to follow a familiar architecture. A funder commissions a study. Consultants design instruments, often drawing on frameworks developed elsewhere. Communities are engaged — genuinely, in many cases — but primarily as data sources. The analysis happens offsite. The report travels upward, to the funder, to the institution, to the policy process. Rarely does it travel back.

This architecture is not incidental. It reflects assumptions about where knowledge is produced and validated, about whose interpretation of a situation is authoritative, and about what the purpose of evidence actually is. When monitoring, evaluation, and learning systems are designed primarily to satisfy donor reporting requirements, the learning function becomes secondary by design. Compliance replaces inquiry. Activity tracking replaces insight.

The result is that communities living on the frontlines of climate change — whose knowledge of local risk, seasonal change, and adaptive practice is sophisticated and specific — are systematically excluded from the analytical process that is supposed to serve them.

Communities living on the frontlines of climate change carry knowledge that no institutional dataset can fully capture. Their exclusion from analysis is not an oversight. It is a design choice.

— Yakhuluntu Dubazana, Bantu-Edge

What I Observed in the Field

Working on the ACRC Climate Change Cross-Cutting Report, synthesising climate-related findings from twelve cities across sub-Saharan Africa, what struck me was not the absence of data. There was data. What was missing was the connective tissue between what communities already knew and what institutions were willing to act on.

In Durban, communities in coastal and informal settlement areas could describe changes in flooding patterns, storm intensity, and access to resources with a specificity that institutional risk models had not yet fully captured. That knowledge existed. It was not inaccessible. But the research architecture had not been designed to hold it.

In the just energy transition work in Mpumalanga — research exploring social vulnerabilities in coal-dependent communities — the gap showed up differently. Communities had clear, articulate views about what a just transition would require. But the policy processes designed to support transition were moving to timelines and logics that bore little relationship to community readiness, livelihood cycles, or the social fabric of towns built entirely around a single extractive industry.

In both cases, the problem was not a lack of engagement. It was that engagement had been designed as a procedural step rather than an epistemic one — something you did to satisfy a methodology, not something you did because communities hold knowledge that should fundamentally shape analysis.

The MEL Problem

Monitoring, evaluation, and learning systems are where this disconnection becomes most consequential — and most fixable.

Most MEL frameworks in climate and development programmes are designed around donor-defined indicators, output tracking, and periodic reporting cycles. These are not inherently bad. Accountability matters. But when the MEL system is the only system — when there is no parallel infrastructure for learning, adaptation, and course correction — programmes lose their capacity to respond to what is actually happening on the ground.

A technically excellent baseline study delivered in a format that no implementing partner can act on is not a successful baseline study. It is a missed opportunity.

— Bantu-Edge MEL Approach

A programme designed for climate adaptation in a specific ecological and social context needs a MEL system that can hold complexity — one that tracks not just whether activities were delivered, but whether the programme's theory of change is holding, whether assumptions about risk and resilience are being confirmed or challenged, and whether the communities it serves are experiencing meaningful change in their adaptive capacity.

Designing for Use, Not Just Rigour

The question I now bring to every research and MEL assignment is simple: who needs to use this, and how?

It sounds obvious. In practice, it is surprisingly rare as a genuine design constraint. Research is often designed for rigour — for methodological defensibility, for academic credibility, for donor satisfaction. These are legitimate goals. But they are not sufficient. Evidence must travel beyond the report.

Small group discussion at the Gender Equal and Inclusive Just Transition workshop — participants developing their own climate action plans. Facilitation designed for dialogue, not delivery. It means building feedback mechanisms into programme design so that learning shapes implementation in real time. It means treating community knowledge not as background context but as analytical input that can challenge and sharpen institutional understanding.

In Southern African contexts specifically, this requires attending carefully to the political economy of knowledge — to who is trusted, whose voice carries authority, and what institutional incentives shape how evidence is received.

What This Means for Climate and Development Work

The climate crisis in Southern Africa is not primarily a data deficit problem. We have research. We have models. We have detailed assessments of risk, vulnerability, and adaptive capacity across multiple geographies and sectors.

What we have less of is the connective infrastructure that moves evidence into decision-making — that makes research usable by the institutions and communities it is designed to serve.

Building that infrastructure is not glamorous work. It requires attention to process, to relationships, to the design of systems that most programme budgets treat as administrative rather than strategic. But it is arguably the most important investment a climate or development programme can make.

Because in the end, the question is not whether the research was rigorous. The question is whether anything changed.
